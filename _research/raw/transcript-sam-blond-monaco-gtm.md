## 1. Scaling Brex to $12B

**Turner:** Sam, welcome to the show. You're most well known for scaling Brex to what the headlines say was about $12 billion. I thought it'd be fun to do a deep dive on the modern GTM playbook — the world's changed a little since you did that. What has changed the most in the sales process over the past couple of years?

**Sam:** Thanks for having me. For context, I left Brex in early 2022, and my entire experience there — roughly 2018 to 2022 — was pre-AI. Then I spent time at Founders Fund, started a bit of an incubation there, and Monaco became its own independent business. Things are quite different from both 2018, when I joined Brex, and 2022, when I left.

## 2. How AI speeds up prospecting and TAM building

**Sam:** There are specific sales workflows that AI and agents are just better than humans at — the things that are fully online. An example: building your TAM based off your ICP. You're a company, you want to sell to a bunch of companies, you have an idea of what that list looks like — let's go create a database of all the companies we want to sell to.

Historically that's called prospecting. A lot of salespeople — and founders, pre-sales-hires — spent their time going on LinkedIn, finding company types, logging into databases, running lists, filtering and exporting data, manipulating lists, adding employee counts, locations, verticals and sub-verticals.

**Turner:** So they're manually going through a spreadsheet — maybe some tools give you a dump, but you're filtering it down by hand.

**Sam:** Right. You just want a database of companies you can sell to, and historically it's an iterative process that takes a lot of time.

**Turner:** What percentage of the sales process was this?

**Sam:** If you were dedicated to outbound — an SDR — a meaningful percentage of your time when I was at Brex, pre-AI, was finding new companies to reach out to. The outreach was actually the easier part: you drop someone in a pre-written sequence. Finding the right people at the right time took a lot of time and labor.

So there's this concept of building your TAM — finding all the companies you can sell to, then **scoring your accounts**, because not every company is created equal. Take Monaco or Brex: you're selling to startups, but not all startups are equal. Are they in San Francisco? We may want to prioritize an account headquartered in SF. There's a sweet spot for employee count. Business model — sales-led or product-led growth? Dynamics that influence how you prioritize the account. Historically a very manual process.

Then you **overlay signals**: are they visiting the website, hiring for a certain role? You can do this with a human, but agents are just better at it today.

**Turner:** You might literally spend 10 minutes scrolling a careers page to see if they're hiring for a role — that happens in a second with AI.

**Sam:** You can have an agent crawl every website in your entire database in basically real time. Then **finding the buyers**: this startup, we sell to the sales leader — who is it, what's their email? That used to be manual. All of this can now be done in near-zero time, and it's super high-leverage in terms of how salespeople and founders spend their time.

## 3. Using AI to get more leverage

**Sam:** The right way to think about how AI impacts go-to-market: it is **not a silver bullet**. It gives founders and salespeople leverage to spend their time on the things AI is less good at. Two big categories:

First, **customer-facing work and developing relationships**. You should get leverage from agents creating meetings from this database, writing sequences, targeting people at the right time — so you spend more of your time customer-facing. Agents are not good at that. People still want to buy from people.

Second, **creative brand or demand-gen campaigns** — things requiring creativity, ingenuity, and operational complexity. Billboards: it's difficult for AI to come up with that as a concept, do the creative, work with the billboard company. Or a launch — a funding round, a product launch. AI might give you ideas, but the orchestration, creating the video, distributing it on social — those are very high-ROI uses of human labor today. We can spend more time on that because AI gives us leverage in the areas where we historically had to spend so much time.

## 4. Incubating Monaco at Founders Fund

**Turner:** You'd led successful sales teams, and you decided to start this AI-native sales company. What made you decide this was the highest opportunity to work on?

**Sam:** I sort of fell into it. I was at Founders Fund doing investing, and Founders Fund has a track record of incubating companies — Palantir, Anduril, Scott has General Matter, Delian has Varda. At the time, maybe 2023, not a lot of capital was being deployed — still a hangover from 2022. There's only one type of technology company I'm qualified to found, as a non-technical founder: a go-to-market / sales technology company.

Thinking about the space broadly: we're in the relatively early, trending toward middle, innings of a platform shift through which a new market leader will emerge. The market leader in GTM tech is Salesforce, which benefited from the on-prem-to-cloud platform shift. AI rhymes with that — it's an architecture-level decision whether you're AI-native or pre-AI. So: now is the time to found a sales technology company, and the function is ripe for disruption.

**Turner:** A year, year and a half ago, there was a lot of "the big incumbent has all this distribution, they'll just add AI features and win." I don't know if that's happened. Did you think through how the market would evolve?

**Sam:** A couple of things are true. The market-leading category today is CRM. We're going to invent a new category — we think CRM is the legacy thing, a reactive database people used to work in when I was at Brex and prior companies. Now there's something oriented around **revenue automation**. We're not just disrupting the IT budget or the software layer; we're going after the **labor**. We're far more outcome-oriented and labor-disruptive than the reactive database that was the enterprise software layer.

We believe the future market leader will be significantly larger than today's, because the winner isn't just going after the IT budget — it's the labor budget, which is arguably larger. Which vendor wins? Hard to predict. It's certainly possible it's Salesforce.

## 5. Innovator's dilemma in AI

**Sam:** But the incumbents — Salesforce, HubSpot, others — face an innovator's dilemma. They have existing customers and revenue on a platform architected pre-AI and pre-agents. They can keep serving those customers and improving that platform, or start from scratch and disrupt themselves with a truly AI-native platform. So far the decision has been to overlay AI on top of a pre-AI system architecture. Better than no AI, but worse than first-principles thinking: if you built this from scratch, would you do it differently? Of course. You wouldn't architect a platform with a UI reactive to user input and then have an agent pretend to be the human doing the input.

**Turner:** It also depends how the product is sold — steak dinners with an innovation committee versus a guy who just needs to start using it and have it work. Those product decisions look completely different.

**Sam:** Maybe eventually it's all of the above. My version: the disruption and evolution of the market is a bit Darwinian. We're like Salesforce in the prior era — starting with a narrow, almost niche segment, which for us is technology startups. We're already executing on building a better product for that very narrow segment. Salesforce's revenue is not concentrated in early-stage startups, so right now we're not that disruptive to that very large business. But we'll move upmarket, and outside technology startups. It starts with a narrow segment where we get as close to a monopoly as possible, then organically expand. The really large enterprises are very difficult to get off their system of record — Salesforce specifically. So this is a many-years-long journey that starts with transactional sales to founders and over time gets to steak dinners with committees considering the next five-year technology implementation.

## 6. AI companies should build full platforms, not wedge products

**Turner:** When you describe it as a revenue engine — every dollar of GDP is revenue, really. Everything needs to make money, so the runway of how big it gets is basically the global economy.

**Sam:** You should be my hype man for VCs. Two things come to mind. One: with revenue generation, the outcomes are very objective. We're building the platform to drive toward outcomes — inputs like meetings generated and conversion rates, but the outcome of that equation is revenue and customer growth. Measuring the success of a platform like Monaco is relatively easy when you're oriented around customer acquisition and revenue growth.

And if you think about where so much of the global workforce sits — sales is one of the most common functions. If you can disrupt that global workforce, it's far larger than, say, support. You can apply that to legal or finance — there are just more salespeople than any of those positions in the world.

**Turner:** There are all these new AI-native products being created — customer support, CRM, a bunch of them. What are you uniquely doing that makes Monaco stand out?

**Sam:** There's one intuitive application of AI in a product like Monaco: within the product, agents — effectively paying for compute — do the work humans used to do. Putting labor into the software. That's the intuitive premise of AI in enterprise software, and we're certainly doing that.

The less intuitive thing we were deliberate about from early on is the impact AI has on our ability to **build product quickly** — which manifests in the **breadth** of product we decided to build. Historically, in this category especially, there were lots of point-solution companies. Conventional wisdom: find a problem, build a solution, use it as a wedge, expand from there. We took the exact opposite approach, with the belief that the cost of building software is trending to zero.

Monaco is a very broad platform. We're not just replacing traditional CRM — we're replacing as many of the point solutions that historically integrated over APIs as we can, and we'll keep biting off as much as possible, because of how much faster technology companies can build software now. The more all-in-one and deeply integrated, the more you can accomplish from one platform — that's better over time. We started with that mindset from day one.

Others started narrow — "we're the AI CRM," "AI for outbound," "AI for follow-ups." Our belief is those should be **features of a much broader platform**, not independent products and companies. Why is this true today and not historically? Because of how quickly you can build. Part of our moat is the breadth of the platform from day one.

**Turner:** So the argument is: it's so easy to build that the "you need to build all this stuff for a fully-fledged suite" objection isn't a big deal anymore.

**Sam:** And don't you want to partner with one vendor and get everything from the same place? By being end-to-end across the customer lifecycle, you don't have data in different silos like you do with a hub and a bunch of spokes — point solutions around it. And you can leverage complex workflows: if you're an outbound-only product, you have no insight into ACVs, what's converting, how customers perform over time, who we just closed, how to feed that data point back to the top of the funnel. With one data plane and one suite of functionality from the same product, there are real tailwinds — you can do things you can't with the point-solution hub model.

**Turner:** Right — certain messaging converting best, feeding back analytics, what product is used most in the first 30 minutes, the heaviest use case — maybe you emphasize that in outbound or onboarding. The two pieces of sales: increasing demand gen and conversion. Anything you do to increase those is the whole point.

## 7. Revenue is just a math equation

**Sam:** You nailed it — it's a math equation. Revenue has three variables: opportunities (or leads) × conversion rates × ACV. If you just want customer count, remove the price. How many opportunities are we getting, what's our conversion rate? And tying back to why we went broad from the start: generating opportunities and increasing conversion rates can play off one another.

## 8. Two ways AI increases conversion rates

**Sam:** I'll give two examples that were historically RevOps insights requiring a BCG- or McKinsey-style analyst spending a lot of time running reports, cutting data, checking whether the data matched the anecdotes. Companies wouldn't realize these insights until they were a couple hundred employees.

**Turner:** Why was it so hard? The data was scattered?

**Sam:** The way it starts: a founder closes some deals, hires a few salespeople, then a sales leader, who hires more salespeople. It's not in any of their DNA to think hard about cutting data in many ways to identify trends.

Two specific examples — one from Zenefits, one from Brex — that we can now learn in near real time. At Zenefits, we were doing health insurance, regulated by state. You had demos on your calendar and nobody paid attention to "this demo is in Florida — what does that mean for the outcome?" What we found was that the thing influencing conversion rates more than anything was **where the company was headquartered**, because of the dynamics of the insurance space.

At Brex: we could sell both to finance people and to accounting/controllers. A rep would show up on a call and not note whether they were talking to a CFO, a VP Finance, an FP&A person, a controller, or a chief accounting officer. What we found: **finance people converted at ~4x the rate of controllers.** Once we understood it, it made perfect sense — controllers wanted to go deep on expense mapping, near-technical accounting conversations reps weren't equipped to have. Finance people asked: what's our rebate, our credit limit, our float? Exactly what salespeople are good at.

The point: agents can find these things today. We have an **insights agent**, trained to cut data every possible way — buyer, location, vertical and sub-vertical, segment and sub-segment — and see when you reach statistically significant information worth surfacing to a customer. Then the next step is logical: we oriented all our sales resources around the states with the highest conversion rates; we oriented all first touches toward finance personas. Everything else stays constant, and you've materially influenced conversion rates by changing top-of-funnel action based on the insight.

And again — really difficult to accomplish that full workflow if you're just doing outbound, or just the system of record needing a third party to orchestrate outbound. That's the advantage of being broad from day one.

**Turner:** You might be a great outbounder, but there are so many things outside your influence — one tweak earlier in the system you never get to touch and don't even know exists.

**Sam:** There's an age-old debate I'm on the other side of from most people: do you compensate an SDR on meetings booked — which they fully control — or on the revenue those meetings generate? I land on revenue. Maybe it's not one-size-fits-all. But the AI era lets us orient only around the outcome we're driving toward: revenue. What are the characteristics of companies that are **closing**, to apply back to targeting — not the characteristics of companies we can get a meeting with, regardless of what happens after.

**Turner:** Then you can use that downstream data — who's actually closing — to book meetings that are more likely to close.

**Sam:** There are really fun things you can do with this. We had reps who sold far better to founders than to finance people — maybe they were networky, hosted events, knew a bunch of founders, were in the founder community. So beyond insights at the customer-type or persona level, you get insights at the **rep level**: this rep converts certain opportunity types — company size is a common one, which feeds when you should segment and who goes in which segment. AI can then route the meeting to whoever has the highest probability of closing it.

**Turner:** So you can dynamically shift your sales team — like dynamic pricing for Uber or airlines, but dynamic allocation of staff.

**Sam:** You can gamify everything, all oriented around outcomes: how do we close the most customers and the most revenue. And it's totally objective — not the sales leader favoring someone with more opportunities. It's all AI.

## 9. AI will never replace spending time with customers

**Turner:** Anything else that's changed — or stayed the same?

**Sam:** There's something a little dangerous about introducing AI into go-to-market, probably more with founders than with sales organizations. We have to keep reminding folks: AI is not the silver bullet for GTM — Monaco or any tool. You're not automating away go-to-market so you can spend your time elsewhere. There's a risk you leverage AI too much and remove yourself from the incredibly high-ROI things you have to do as a founder.

What's stayed the same: there is **no higher-ROI use of founder time** — especially post-product-market-fit, pre-scaled sales org — **than being customer-facing**. AI doesn't automate away meeting prospective and current customers. In fact, the inverse — maybe slightly contrarian — AI enables you to spend **more** time customer-facing.

Zoom is great, but when you can meet a customer face to face — especially if deal sizes warrant it — do it. We sell to startups in San Francisco, so we default to in-person when convenient. We're not getting on a plane to meet a New York founder for a $25K ACV deal — it doesn't support it. But if you're selling mid-market-plus into the enterprise, especially a marquee account: get on the plane. That has not changed, and the ROI is incredibly high.

## 10. Don't measure the impact of brand marketing

**Sam:** We invest a lot in what would be categorized as marketing — brand marketing, even demand-gen marketing. People try to get too scientific about measuring the impact of this stuff at an early stage.

Examples of our campaigns: billboards, launch videos for fundraise announcements and product launches, gifting campaigns — we send poker sets, pretty cool ones, we pay maybe $100 each. We throw poker tournaments — the **Monaco Invitational**. Founders love poker tournaments; we give away cash to the winners and throw a party.

The obvious measurement questions: how many customers signed up who attended the poker event? What did the poker sets cost and how many recipients signed up? Billboards are even harder to attribute.

Here's what's less understood, and why we lean in so hard. When we do outbound now to founders, our reply rates today — **same company, same product, same message** — are exponentially higher than before we launched into public beta. We were unknown, the way all startups are definitionally unknown at first. A big part of why our reply rates are so high is the brand campaigns: there's now name and brand recognition.

Two things are very hard to quantify with a gifting campaign, a billboard campaign, an event: the impact on **outbound efficacy** — reply rates, meeting rates ("oh, I see your billboards everywhere," "I heard about your event, I'd love to come to the next one") — and the impact on **conversion rates**, because you have inherent credibility. If you have brand recognition and people are talking about you, someone is more likely to feel comfortable moving forward with you.

**Turner:** It's even the group chat: "hey, we just signed up for Monaco" — "I got a demo, anyone else use it?" — and six people go "I've seen their billboards," "I went to the poker tournament." Multiple touchpoints you can't measure — you don't even know they're happening.

**Sam:** You nailed it. I get asked all the time: how do you measure the impact of the Monaco Invitational? The billboards? The launch videos? The real answer is: **we don't.** You will spend more time and effort trying to measure, and the outcome won't be accurate — there's so much information you don't have that your takeaways will guide you in the wrong direction. The **anecdotes are more valuable than the data points** — the things people regularly bring up to you.

And we've failed at things. A lot of my learnings came from creative campaigns we tried at Brex. One that wasn't successful: we opened a restaurant — South Park Cafe — a co-working space and a real restaurant serving lunch and dinner. We were maybe 50 employees and the restaurant had 20–30. Running a restaurant is hard. The lesson isn't "don't open the restaurant" — the lesson is you should be trying a bunch of stuff, and some of it won't work. We could tell from the anecdotes it wasn't driving impact. Chalk it up as a win that you tried, take the learning, cross it out, move to the next thing. Once you're at today's Brex scale, of course you start measuring. But at ~50 employees: try a bunch of stuff, anecdotally understand what's working, double and triple down on that.

**Turner:** One of my best marketing stunts: a fake VC pitch competition on TikTok. I made a video with a filter messing up my face and voice, pretending to be a VC listening to your pitch — "what do you do again? who introduced us? wait, what's your TAM?" — while arguing with my gardener on the phone. The classic terrible founder pitching experience. A hundred people stitched it pitching me, people posted it on Twitter — probably a million views across everything. It took me half an hour and it's the video people mention to me the most.

**Sam:** Hard to measure, right? There's the objective part — likes, views — and then the anecdotes: people mention it to you all the time. And it lasts. Maybe there's a 2.0 of that you could bring back.

**Turner:** The thing I've been doing is hosting comedy shows — we hire really good comedians, Netflix-special level. It's hard to do, but people love it.

**Sam:** That's really smart — when I hear something like that I immediately think: we could totally host one.

## 11. Your marketing must be different (and hard)

**Turner:** Most events — a dinner, a panel about AI — you're like "do I really have to go?" Poker is interesting because people love poker. A comedy show — "I haven't been to one in a while, this will probably be fun, I'll bring my girlfriend" — you actually want to go, and you remember it.

**Sam:** You have to do things that are **different**. At some scale dinners probably matter, with a special guest speaker — but it's not creative, lots of companies do dinners, and the bar is "really cool restaurant" or people won't show up. That's why I took note of the comedy thing: people love to laugh, and you don't see startups or VCs hosting comedy shows. We could even iterate — do a roast of a prominent figure who's up for being made fun of.

One thing I want to come back to: "yeah, but you've raised a bunch of money, and everything you're describing is expensive." Some truth to that. If you're bootstrapped or seed-stage and not spending six figures on billboards, there's a process: **every single month, force yourself to come up with a creative idea**. Meet as a group, whiteboard, vote on the best idea. A comedy show is a great one — you could do it in an office, get a couple of amateur-to-professional comedians for not much money.

**Turner:** For ours, we used Merge's office in New York. My friend Alexis Gay — kind of a professional corporate comedian, B2B comedy — helped me plan and run it and brought friends, tech employees doing comedy on the side. We paid them a little. We basically paid for food and a bit for the comedians — a couple grand total.

**Sam:** Perfect example of something fun you can do cheaply. There are two things to orient around when you don't have a big budget. The things that cost a lot — third-party advertising, paid ads, billboards — are easy: you literally enter your credit card. That's why everyone does it. **Force yourself to come up with the creative, sometimes operationally complex things.**

The poker sets: retail maybe $180, we paid $100. Send those to 100 founders, you're at $10,000 of spend — very targeted, and you're giving something genuinely cool. Try doing that on LinkedIn or Google — the money disappears fast. And imagine being the recipient: would you rather the advertiser pay Google to track you, or get the poker set? You want the poker set. Do things that stand out. It doesn't have to break the bank — set budgets per campaign. The worst thing to do is nothing at all.

**Turner:** And physical things versus Google ads: I'm sure I've gotten ads, I just don't remember them. If I get shipped a big package with a poker set, whether I use it or not, I will always remember it happened. There's one guy who keeps sending me stuff — my wife is in on it now, she jokes about it. I talk about it to people.

**Sam:** You just said something insightful: **the thing could be better.** My cliché saying: it's NOT the thought that counts. Do not send a t-shirt with your startup's logo to someone you want to sell to. The gift actually matters — it can be negative value if it's some chachki. The bar is high: would *you* genuinely think this is cool, setting aside your bias?

One other takeaway — a thought exercise you can actually measure: of all your marketing dollars, have a meaningful percentage — 30%, 50% — that **directly benefits the target customer** rather than a third-party advertiser. Gifting, the poker tournament where people come and have a great time — versus billboards, which we also do. How many of the dollars we spend benefit the people we're marketing to, versus the third parties marketing to them?

**Turner:** One of my favorites: Greptile, AI code review. He sent Greptile-branded energy drinks to engineers. Super tangible — you're up all night coding, drink the energy drink, maybe check out the code review.

**Sam:** Greptile is great — Daksh is the CEO, they're a Monaco customer. They seem to be crushing it.

## 12. Customer discovery calls and working with design partners

**Turner:** Should you be doing all this branding before you have a product? Before you have customers? What order — talk to customers first?

**Sam:** I'll speak to the n-of-1 experience of building Monaco, with the qualification that unlike the sales topics, I have less experience on when to start brand spend. So I'll just tell you what we did.

We were building for about a year, deliberately in stealth. During that year, the thing that gave us growing confidence was **regular customer interview / feedback sessions**. We'd schedule a discovery call, pitch a bit of what we were building toward — founder or salesperson on the other side — and get feedback on the concept.

**Turner:** With existing customers or potential?

**Sam:** We didn't have a product to sell — it was discovery with the potential customer base. Educational, and at minimum it reinforced that we were building in the right direction. We built a very broad, compound startup, replacing a bunch of tools — one downside is it takes longer to build, even with AI and codegen. We didn't want to build in the dark for a year.

Then — a little contrarian — during the **design customer phase**, about six months with ~15 design partners, **we didn't charge.** The real value to our business in that phase was improving the product, not generating revenue. Charging would have been friction against the thing we really wanted: feedback. We did, though, get people to commit that they would actually make this their platform of record — not use it alongside another tool. Buy-in and commitment that we'd build this together.

**Turner:** So they weren't on it yet, but promised that in 3 months when ready, they'd switch?

**Sam:** They switched over when it was ready for the design phase — rough around the edges, but with services accompanying it. Up until our public beta launch in February, we operated in stealth: design partners, not charging, getting feedback, getting the product from day one of the design phase to launch-ready. **We spent zero dollars on marketing.** We did outbound to test the product and acquire some design customers; the rest came through introductions — investors, employees, personal networks.

## 13. The zero to 100 launch

**Sam:** We really wanted the coming-out-of-stealth launch. We had switched from zero brand investment — in fact we invested in NOT having a brand: LinkedIns said "company in stealth," the website said "coming soon." Then we wanted the **zero-to-100 shotgun blast**: on launch day, February 11, everything happens at once — poker sets delivered, billboards up, launch video out, tons of outbound going — going from totally unknown to some level of brand recognition in our target market as fast as possible.

**Turner:** Why the zero to 100? A lot of people post publicly pre-launch — "looking for design customers," website up, but no big launch.

**Sam:** First, a reason NOT to — this is probably the wrong approach for most companies. What enabled us, true at Brex too for different reasons: **we didn't need to be known to acquire the initial customers** needed to get the product to launch quality. Brex was a YC company — they built the initial product for their batch, batchmates who couldn't get credit cards; word of mouth let them build in stealth. For us, outbound was working, and I could offer to be a GTM advisor to design partners — a complementary skill set many founders don't have. If you can't acquire design customers through network effects or something other than being known, it'll be very difficult to get people to use your service without even a website. That was a luxury we had.

The benefit of doing it: the frog-in-boiling-water analogy, maybe imperfect. Monaco launched about three months ago. Imagine we'd spread all this over the preceding nine months — you lose the "all of a sudden I'm seeing this thing everywhere" effect. Everything orients around growth: you go from zero to very fast growth, versus the clock ticking from the design phase onward, where it's harder to make a big splash because people have kind of already heard of you. Our growth trajectory went *like this* instead of *like this*.

**Turner:** There are also products I saw at first launch and thought "honestly not that great," skipped past, and that perception stuck — a year later it's a great product and I never noticed. When you launched, I remember seeing the product and thinking: this looks really robust.

**Sam:** Thank you. There's a related topic — how do you know when you're ready to launch? It's another "we don't measure" one. It's a sensation. We think about company building in phases: design customer phase (basically private beta), then **public beta** — we're metering who comes in, waitlist for companies not right in the strike zone of who we can make successful — then **GA**, which should come in July. Public beta bought us time and goodwill: brand recognition starts, while the folks we work with understand we're still early. GA is what we should really earn our reputation around. We're fortunate to have incredible customers who really love us — most of our revenue today comes from referrals, which is maybe the data point that says it best.

## 14. Monaco's launch playbook

**Turner:** Walk us through the launch playbook — you posted about it, we'll link it.

**Sam:** First — and maybe before that — **you can launch a bunch of times.**

**Turner:** That's one of my favorite things, when someone says "we're launching again." You don't even have to say "again."

**Sam:** We did our public beta launch with a very product-centric video and announced our Series A, which amplified it — TechCrunch covered the A. The Series B announcement a few weeks ago almost came off like a launch. And we'll do the GA launch. That's three launches in roughly five months, February 11 to mid-July. So the first qualification: product launch, fundraise launch — keep taking advantage of these point-in-time opportunities for attention and amplification.

The **table-stakes** thing, especially for a product launch: I like the video format, plus a deliberate social strategy around distribution. The content — the post, the video — matters; **distribution matters equally**. The way we do it: a spreadsheet with four tabs — employees, investors, friends of the firm, customers. When you launch, you track and do outreach to each group, both the day before and day of. With employees, ask: who are the three to five most influential people in your network, or with the largest followings? Add them to the friends-of-the-firm tab. Everyone should be doing that.

Then I like **launch campaigns**. We did the poker set deliveries, and we had trucks driving around San Francisco with LED sides and backs.

**Turner:** Tell that story — it was a Super Bowl hangover thing?

**Sam:** I was out for a run — more realistically a walk — the Saturday before the Super Bowl, and as far as the eye could see there were these LED box trucks I'd never seen in San Francisco, all advertising gambling companies for the Super Bowl. We were launching February 11, the Wednesday after. I crossed the street, met one of the truck drivers, got the company's card, started calling around — and we took advantage of the excess inventory already in San Francisco. The trucks would otherwise have had to drive in from Vegas or LA with all those direct costs, so we got a crazy discount on trucks that stayed.

Beyond that, the process — I've done this with a few of our customers: **45 days before launch, assemble a launch committee** — might be your whole five-person company. Meet Friday afternoon; the weekend assignment is everyone brings two to three ideas for the launch. In a lot of ways, the crazier the better — nothing off limits, maybe set budget constraints per campaign. Whiteboard everything, leave the room with the three or four best ideas, and execute them.

**Turner:** And these are usually offline, separate from the launch post itself?

**Sam:** Generally separate. A customer of ours we love — Judgment Labs, we're mutual customers — had their launch, and we did a couple of workshops in their office. ## 17. Founders should send early outbound

**Turner:** We've talked a lot about pre-launch outbound. How does outbound change after you launch?

**Sam:** The opinions I'll express here are ingrained into the platform itself. When you get started, a few things matter. **Who sends the outbound is very important.** Even at startups with early salespeople, you want the origination to come from the founder. Founders get higher reply rates than early salespeople, because recipients know they're going to get sold to if it's a salesperson.

Other things that matter: **timing**, and the **mediums** — you want to be multi-channel, at least LinkedIn and email, not just spraying the universe with cold email through domains that aren't your real domain. You'll have far better efficacy multi-channel at the same time, as part of the same sequence. And **message and sequence structure** really matter — how many touchpoints, how the message is structured. Nothing earth-shattering, but it's already set up for you [in Monaco]. Over time you expand beyond founders sending the outbound.

You also want to be targeted about prioritizing companies with certain characteristics — for us, primarily San Francisco-based founders, which plays into our SF campaigns right now.

**Turner:** Why is the founder sending it so important? Because multi-channel means they can click and see who's originating?

**Sam:** That's exactly it — it gives credibility to the person reaching out. The founder is the most trusted person at that company. It's founder-to-founder — not necessarily "I'm selling you something," even though it really is — more of a trusted sale, or maybe you'll learn something talking to this other founder.

And you can be **more creative with messaging**. One of our customers, Parley — a YC company doing AI for immigration law, selling into immigration law firms. Phil, the CEO, structures the message something like: "I started Parley after watching my father for years…" — maybe missing family events because he was buried in docs. You can see where I'm going —

**Turner:** Way more relatable. A salesperson could never say that.

**Sam:** Exactly. You can orient the message around the company's origination story — a layer of credibility, plus content only a founder can articulate — and it leads to significantly higher reply rates.

## 18. How multi-channel augments AI outbound

**Turner:** We can all sympathize with the AI email slop out there. How do I navigate that if I'm the one sending AI-generated emails? How do you get around people deleting your email without opening it?

**Sam:** Here's how I think about it as an evolution. Old movie — Glengarry Glen Ross, the '80s — they used the Yellow Pages to cold call, and there were the quality leads they could never get to. This concept — identify a potential buyer by company or individual, target them to buy your thing — has existed for many decades. It evolved from Yellow Pages and phone calls (and door-knocking before that) to email. When I joined EchoSign, it was pre-SDR-outreach tools — pre-Outreach, pre-SalesLoft, the tools that let SDRs do marketing automation the way Marketo had before.

**Turner:** So every email you got was literally handwritten by someone.

**Sam:** Certainly the ones I was sending in 2007–2008: copy-paste, the body stays the same, plug in "Hi Turner" at the top, over and over. Then Outreach was first, I think, to do marketing-automation-style sending from an SDR — suddenly I could send 200 a day just by throwing contacts into a sequence.

The point: there was this meme for a while — "outbound is dead," "cold email is dead." **It is not dead. It's evolving.** If you drop thousands of email addresses into an outbound tool with the same templated copy, no brand, the recipient doesn't know you or your company, your website kind of sucks — they're not going to reply. Your reply rates will be 0.0-whatever percent.

There IS an approach that's effective today — and without talking my own book too much, the reply rates we're seeing at Monaco are higher than I ever saw with Outreach-style outbound. It leverages AI, **intent signals** for why you reach out and what you say, custom messages. And it will be different in 2030 — you have to stay up with the times.

**Turner:** The thing I see a lot: "quick question" as the subject. Lowercase subjects generally seem to work. "RE:" —

**Sam:** Reply. Yeah, those do well.

**Turner:** Anything where it doesn't seem AI-generated, I open it and go "they got me again." But the multi-channel thing really matters — message them on LinkedIn AND email them?

**Sam:** Yes. And depending on the industry, you also call them — yourself or a service — as a third channel. Gifting can be a fourth. The table stakes are LinkedIn and email. It's not one plus one equals two — it's one plus one equals four. You can say "following up from my message on LinkedIn," and they go "I did see this person's message." You tie it all back together.

**Turner:** One big mistake: just following up on email — "hey, just following up, did you see this?" I feel like the better one is "hey, we just launched a new feature, check it out" — you don't even acknowledge you're following up, you keep adding value. Bad one or good one?

**Sam:** Piggybacking off what you said — [the "just following up"] is not a good one. Sometimes it works, but at this stage it's reached diminishing returns. Everyone knows it's automated. You don't feel the psychological "this person keeps reaching out, I should let them know" — it's not real.

**Turner:** I'll usually respond to those only if it's someone I know in person and will see again — "not interested." Otherwise never, because you could be fake. ## 19. Using intent signals and outreach timing to increase conversions

**Sam:** There are things that really do work — and there's probably a moral in this — they work because **they're actually relevant.** Examples of intent signals that benefit the recipient:

"I see you have a job posting for this role — we actually automate what that role does." Say someone posts for an executive assistant; that's a reasonable time for an AI-EA company to reach out: "Saw this posting [hyperlink to the job posting]. Want to try us for one week, free? If we don't work, keep your search going." That will convert — not 100% of the time, but far more than randomly blasting everyone about your AI EA.

Another example — we have a customer called Nowadays, AI event planning. One of their intent signals: "agent, crawl the internet for blog posts about a company kickoff or recent offsite." Then: "Saw your blog post — we can help you plan the next one." You're reaching the right person at the company — the one who planned the thing — and it's top of mind because they wrote a blog post about it. These dramatically increase the likelihood of a reply relative to sending 10,000 identical emails to everyone in your TAM. If you're a founder, you should be leveraging these.

**Turner:** The one that always gets me: "I listened to your podcast episode with Sam, really liked the part about intent signals" — they suck up to you, and you're like, "man, I have to at least acknowledge this."

**Sam:** I receive those too. Better than a template — but it works when the **personalization is relevant**. "I see your job posting for an EA — we are literally an AI EA." The alternative: "Saw you're from Kansas City, go Chiefs — are you thinking about finance workflow automation?" I'm actually more averse to that than to just telling me about the finance thing you build.

**Turner:** Right — "hey, I also have a brother who went to Alabama, I love Nick Saban interviews" — then they jump into the pitch. Zero relevance.

## 20. Two common ways founders mess up when scaling revenue

**Turner:** Where do you see founders most mess up when systematizing — going from duct-taped founder sales to a team, a machine?

**Sam:** In sequential order. **Zero customers:** I've seen, a handful of times, founders struggling to acquire customer number one and attributing it to "I don't know how to sell, I'm not a salesperson, so I'll hire one." That is the wrong diagnosis and the wrong solution. **There is no one better in the world at acquiring the first handful of customers than the founder.** If you're not acquiring customers, maybe shift how you present the product — but it's likely a product-market-fit thing. A random third-party salesperson isn't going to change that for you.

Then — true beyond the next phase too — the bottleneck for acquiring customers in four out of five, even nine out of ten companies, is **demand gen**: opportunity creation. And my intuition is that four out of five to nine out of ten founders or early sales leaders **misdiagnose the bottleneck as conversion rates.**

A symptom: early in a month, talking to a founder about why last month missed — "there was this deal I thought would close the last week of the month and it pushed / they went with a competitor… if we'd just converted that, we'd have hit our numbers. We think we know why those deals didn't convert, so we're going to start changing stuff based on that." You're effectively diagnosing the bottleneck as conversion. For nine out of ten startups, the bottleneck is actually **demand**. The problem isn't that you didn't convert that one customer — it's that you didn't have **five** customers in play, where one converting hits your number and two beats it. There's a disproportionate emphasis on conversion rates relative to generating demand.

And **influencing conversion rates is far harder than influencing demand gen.** Thought exercise: you convert 10% of demos. Moving to 15% — you might think that's "increasing conversion by 5%," but it's actually a **50% increase**, and especially at scale that's hard. Versus: last month we had 10 demos; going to 20 — effectively doubling demos — is, not one-size-fits-all, but I suspect far easier than a 50% conversion lift.

**Turner:** For conversion you might need to build a new feature, change the price, go after a different customer…

**Sam:** Yes — or really learn to sell, do discovery well, peel back the onion. There's a lot in it. Or you spend more focus on demand gen, conversion stays constant, and if you double the leads you've effectively doubled sales.

**Turner:** In theory you could just pay more for Google ads and fill the demo list — you probably don't agree.

**Sam:** Here's a version I wouldn't disagree with: **if a channel is working for you in demand gen, double and triple down on it** until you — founder or team — can't take more demos. Until you're at "I now have too many opportunities I'm actively working." Until you've reached what I'd call a **demand-rich environment** — and we're fortunate to have that today — really focus on generating demand.

## 21. Monaco's Forward Deployed AEs

**Turner:** A concept I don't know anyone else doing specifically: the forward-deployed sales executive. What is that at Monaco?

**Sam:** FDE — forward deployed engineer — is the famous acronym; we're **FDAE: forward deployed account executive**. True FDEs are usually for relatively technical products at large enterprises — Palantir, and now OpenAI and Anthropic building their own FDE arms. Monaco's customer is a startup; the agents are technical, but it isn't a highly technical application, and these aren't enterprises needing a BCG or Accenture to map everything out.

Our FDAE does two things. First, they provide a **complementary skill set** to customers who are in founder-led sales right now — an extension of their team helping with messaging, multi-channel, what the message should say. Our reps have deep experience A/B-testing this stuff, in the trenches with founders, alongside the AI. Second, our FDAEs deeply understand how Monaco and its agents operate. If you deploy a demand-gen agent as a founder or sales leader, you have to manage that agent — set it up, program it, check the messaging. **We just do that for you.** It's definitionally not possible for a founder to understand how Monaco works the way a full-time Monaco employee does — so it's far more effective and efficient.

**Turner:** Does anyone else manage and run the agents for customers?

**Sam:** I'm not aware of anyone — doesn't mean no one is. If you're an AI-SDR company with a really large enterprise customer, you should logically provide FDE-style service. But in our category, one of our competitive advantages is the ability to build out a startup go-to-market organization. Most players in the space have more technical or product backgrounds — which is an advantage for understanding the customer and what to build — but we also have an incredible go-to-market organization, the way an experienced engineering-leader-turned-founder could build a great engineering org. Our GTM function at Monaco is a competitive advantage we want to lean into.

**Turner:** It's basically letting your customers leverage that too.

**Sam:** You nailed it. Interestingly, during the A, the biggest objection was "how does this scale?" — the margins. Then the FDAE thing — we were already calling it that — really took off, and in the B it was "we hear this is THE big competitive advantage you have." A bit coincidental in a short time. But yes — it's a big advantage today, and hard for players in the space to replicate or compete with.
